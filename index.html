📏 Measurement
Measurement · Introduction

What is
Measurement?

Measurement is the process of determining the value of a physical quantity by comparing it with a standard unit. For example, when we measure the mass of a bag, we compare it to a kilogram or gram. When we measure the time of a race, we compare it to a second or minute.

📏 Length

How far or long something is.
SI unit: meter (m)

🌡️ Temperature

How hot or cold a substance is.
SI unit: Kelvin (K)

⚖️ Mass

Amount of matter in an object.
SI unit: kilogram (kg)

⏱️ Time

Duration between events.
SI unit: second (s)

💡 Why SI Units?

Scientists use SI units so that measurements are standardized and understood worldwide — making it possible to share, compare, and replicate experiments across different countries.

Measurement · Length

Length &
Distance

Length measures how long or how far something is. The SI unit is the meter (m). Different prefixes are used depending on the scale of measurement.

All Length Units

UnitSymbolIn meters
Kilometerkm1,000 m
Meterm1 m (SI base)
Centimetercm0.01 m
Millimetermm0.001 m
Micrometerµm0.000001 m
Nanometernm0.000000001 m

When to Use Each

  • Kilometer (km) — long distances between cities
  • Meter (m) — height of a person, room dimensions
  • Centimeter (cm) — measuring everyday objects like books
  • Millimeter (mm) — small lengths, thickness of materials
  • Micrometer (µm) — microscopic measurements, cells
  • Nanometer (nm) — nanotechnology, atomic-scale structures

Measuring Instruments

  • Meter rule — everyday lengths
  • Vernier caliper — precise small lengths
  • Micrometer screw gauge — very small lengths
  • Stopwatch — measuring time intervals
  • Thermometer — measuring temperature
Measurement · Temperature

Temperature

Temperature measures how hot or cold something is. The SI unit is Kelvin (K). At the particle level, temperature represents the average kinetic energy of particles — higher temperature = faster-moving particles.

Three Temperature Scales

  • Celsius (°C) — widely used for weather and everyday measurements. Water freezes at 0°C, boils at 100°C.
  • Fahrenheit (°F) — used in the United States. Water freezes at 32°F, boils at 212°F.
  • Kelvin (K) — the SI unit used in all scientific calculations. Starts at absolute zero (0 K).
K = °C + 273
°C = K − 273
°F = (9/5 × °C) + 32

Key Facts

  • Higher temperature = particles move faster
  • 0 K = absolute zero — particles completely stop
  • Kelvin is always required in scientific formulas
  • Temperature ≠ Heat — they are different quantities

💡 Real Life Example

Weather forecasts use °C or °F. But in gas law calculations, scientists must always convert to Kelvin first — because Kelvin starts at true zero particle motion.

Measurement · Mass

Mass

Mass describes the amount of matter in an object. The SI base unit is the kilogram (kg). Unlike weight, mass is constant — it doesn't change with gravity.

All Mass Units

UnitSymbolIn kg
Tont1,000 kg
Kilogramkg1 kg (SI base)
Gramg0.001 kg
Milligrammg0.000001 kg
  • Ton (t) — very heavy objects like vehicles, cargo
  • Kilogram (kg) — everyday objects (people, groceries)
  • Gram (g) — lighter objects (food portions)
  • Milligram (mg) — tiny amounts like medicine doses

Mass vs Weight

  • Mass — amount of matter, constant everywhere, unit: kg
  • Weight — gravitational force on mass, changes with location, unit: Newton (N)
W = m × g

g = 9.8 m/s² on Earth's surface

💡 Example

Your mass is 50 kg on Earth AND on the Moon — mass never changes. But your weight differs: less on the Moon because gravity is weaker there (1.6 m/s² vs 9.8 m/s² on Earth).

Measurement · Conversions

Unit Conversions

All SI prefixes are powers of 10 — making conversions systematic. Always write your units, cancel them step by step, and convert to SI before calculating.

📏 Length

1 km = 1,000 m
1 m = 100 cm
1 cm = 10 mm
1 m = 1,000,000 µm
1 m = 1,000,000,000 nm

⚖️ Mass

1 t = 1,000 kg
1 kg = 1,000 g
1 g = 1,000 mg
1 g = 0.001 kg
1 mg = 0.000001 kg

🌡️ Temperature

K = °C + 273
°C = K − 273
°F = 9/5 × °C + 32
°C = 5/9 × (°F − 32)

⏱️ Time

1 min = 60 s
1 hour = 3,600 s
1 day = 86,400 s

Always convert to SI first!

Example: cm → m

  • 250 cm ÷ 100 = 2.5 m
  • 1.2 m × 100 = 120 cm

Example: kg → g & min → s

  • 3.5 kg × 1000 = 3,500 g
  • 2 min × 60 = 120 s

5. Measurement uncertainty means:

✅ C. There is doubt about the exact value All measurements have some uncertainty due to instrument limitations and human error.

🌡️ Thermal Physics
Thermal Physics · Introduction

What is
Thermal Physics?

Thermal physics is a branch of physics that studies heat, temperature, energy, and how they affect matter. It explains how microscopic particles (atoms and molecules) move and interact, and how this movement relates to macroscopic properties like temperature and pressure.

Thermal physics helps us understand everyday processes such as boiling, freezing, heating systems, engines, and even weather changes.

🌡️ Temperature

Average kinetic energy of particles. Higher temp = faster-moving particles. SI unit: Kelvin (K).

🔥 Heat

Energy transferred between objects due to temperature difference. Flows hot → cold. Unit: Joule (J).

❄️ Latent Heat

Energy to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without changing its temperature.

🌊 Heat Transfer

Three methods: conduction (direct contact), convection (fluid), and radiation (waves).

Thermal Physics · Basics

Temperature
& Heat

Temperature and heat are related but fundamentally different. Temperature is a property of matter; heat is energy in transit between objects due to a temperature difference.

🌡️ Temperature

  • Measures the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance
  • Higher temperature = particles move faster
  • Measured in °C, K, or °F
  • Kelvin (K) is used in all scientific calculations
  • 0 K = absolute zero — no particle motion at all

🔥 Heat

  • Energy transferred due to a temperature difference
  • Always flows from hot → cold
  • Continues until thermal equilibrium is reached
  • Unit of heat: Joule (J)
  • Heat ≠ Temperature — a tiny spark is very hot but carries very little heat
Thermal Physics · SHC

Specific Heat
Capacity

Specific Heat Capacity (SHC) is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C (or 1 K). High SHC = heats slowly; low SHC = heats quickly.

Q = mcΔT

Variables

  • Q = heat energy (Joules, J)
  • m = mass (kg)
  • c = specific heat capacity (J/kg°C)
  • ΔT = change in temperature (°C or K)

Common SHC Values

Water: 4,200 J/kg°C
Aluminium: 900 J/kg°C
Steel/Iron: ~450 J/kg°C
Copper: 385 J/kg°C

Importance of SHC

  • Climate Regulation — water's high SHC (4200 J/kg°C) absorbs enormous heat without large temperature changes, stabilizing Earth's climate
  • Cooling Systems — water used in car radiators and industrial systems absorbs large heat amounts efficiently
  • Cooking — metal pans have low SHC (~450 J/kg°C) so they heat up quickly, making them ideal for cooking
  • Human Body — high water content helps maintain stable body temperature
Thermal Physics · SLH

Specific
Latent Heat

Specific Latent Heat (SLH) is the amount of heat energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without changing its temperature. All energy goes into breaking or forming molecular bonds.

Two Types of SLH

  • Lf — Latent Heat of Fusion
    solid → liquid (melting)
    Ice: 334,000 J/kg
  • Lv — Latent Heat of Vaporization
    liquid → gas (evaporating/boiling)
    Water: 2,260,000 J/kg
Q = mL

Q = heat (J) · m = mass (kg) · L = latent heat (J/kg)

💡 Key Note

During a phase change, temperature stays constant — all heat energy goes into changing state, not raising temperature.

Importance of SLH

  • Cooling — Sweating — when sweat evaporates, it absorbs large amounts of heat from the body due to the high Lv of water, cooling the skin
  • Refrigeration & AC — refrigerators use substances that absorb large latent heat during evaporation to keep food cold
  • Weather & Climate — water evaporation and condensation drive cloud formation, rainfall, and large-scale weather patterns
  • Food Preservation — freezing and melting involve latent heat, helping maintain stable temperatures in cold storage
Thermal Physics · Heat Transfer

Methods of
Heat Transfer

There are three methods of heat transfer. Each works differently and dominates in different materials and situations.

🤝 Conduction

Heat transfer through direct contact. Particles vibrate and pass energy to neighboring particles. Works best in solids — especially metals.

Examples: a metal spoon in hot soup heats up; heat traveling along a rod.

🌀 Convection

Heat transfer through fluid movement. Hot fluid rises (less dense), cool fluid sinks (denser), creating circulation currents. In liquids and gases.

Examples: boiling water, sea breezes, central heating radiators.

☀️ Radiation

Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves. No medium required — can travel through a vacuum at the speed of light.

Examples: sunlight warming Earth, warmth felt from a campfire at distance.

💡 Key Difference

Conduction and convection both require matter to transfer heat. Radiation does NOT — that's why the Sun's energy can reach Earth through the vacuum of space.

Thermal Physics · Gas Laws

Gas Laws

Gas laws describe the relationships between pressure, volume, and temperature for a fixed amount of gas. Fundamental to understanding engines, weather, and the atmosphere.

Boyle's Law

Pressure is inversely proportional to Volume (constant temperature)

  • P↑ when V↓ — squeeze a gas, pressure rises
  • P↓ when V↑ — expand a gas, pressure drops
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

Charles's Law

Volume is directly proportional to Temperature (constant pressure)

  • T↑ = V↑ — heat a gas, it expands
  • T↓ = V↓ — cool a gas, it contracts
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

Ideal Gas Law

Combines Boyle's and Charles's Laws:

PV = nRT
  • P = pressure (Pa)
  • V = volume (m³)
  • n = amount of gas (moles)
  • R = 8.314 J/mol·K (gas constant)
  • T = temperature in Kelvin (K) — always!

💡 Real Life

A bicycle tire feels harder on a hot day — higher temperature increases gas pressure inside at constant volume. Always check tire pressure when tires are cold!

Thermal Physics · Exercises

Exercise
Questions

Click each question to reveal the full worked solution. Try solving it yourself first! 🩷

Q1 — A 2 kg block of aluminum is heated from 25°C to 35°C. Specific heat capacity of aluminum = 900 J/kg°C. Calculate the heat energy absorbed.
Formula: Q = mcΔT Given: m = 2 kg · c = 900 J/kg°C · ΔT = 35 − 25 = 10°C Q = 2 × 900 × 10 ✅ Q = 18,000 J
Q2 — 500 g of water is heated from 20°C to 60°C. Specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J/kg°C. Calculate the heat energy required.
Convert mass: 500 g = 0.5 kg ΔT = 60 − 20 = 40°C Q = mcΔT = 0.5 × 4200 × 40 ✅ Q = 84,000 J
Q3 — Calculate the heat required to melt 3 kg of ice at 0°C. Specific latent heat of fusion of ice = 334,000 J/kg.
Formula: Q = mL Q = 3 × 334,000 ✅ Q = 1,002,000 J = 1.002 × 10⁶ J
Q4 — 2 kg of water at 100°C is converted into steam at 100°C. Specific latent heat of vaporization = 2,260,000 J/kg. Calculate the energy required.
Formula: Q = mL Q = 2 × 2,260,000 ✅ Q = 4,520,000 J = 4.52 × 10⁶ J
Q5 — A 1 kg block of ice at 0°C is completely melted then heated to 20°C. Lf = 334,000 J/kg · c(water) = 4200 J/kg°C. Calculate total heat energy.
Step 1 — Melt ice (0°C solid → 0°C liquid): Q₁ = mL = 1 × 334,000 = 334,000 J Step 2 — Heat water from 0°C to 20°C: Q₂ = mcΔT = 1 × 4200 × 20 = 84,000 J Step 3 — Total: Qtotal = Q₁ + Q₂ = 334,000 + 84,000 ✅ Qtotal = 418,000 J
Q6 — Why does water take longer to heat up compared to metal?
✅ Because water has a higher Specific Heat Capacity (4200 J/kg°C vs ~450 J/kg°C for metals). It requires more heat energy to raise its temperature by 1°C compared to the same mass of metal.
Q7 — Why does sweating cool the body?
✅ Because when sweat evaporates, it absorbs a large amount of heat from the body due to the high Specific Latent Heat of Vaporization of water (2,260,000 J/kg). This removes thermal energy from the skin surface, effectively cooling the body.
